How to Crit

What are some challenges you face when giving a critique?
I‚Äôm a bit worried if I could give him/her a kind of negative feeling. This is primarily because I personally don‚Äôt enjoy talking about negative impressions about the result of other‚Äôs hard work. Rather than thinking that I could help their work improved, I feel like I‚Äôm insulting them. Although I know giving a critique is an important process of synthesis, I just feel so. For example, I have a bad experience that I had to give negative feedback to my peer. When I worked at an exhibition producing company, I had a lot of meetings to plan a Korean calligraphy exhibition. I worked with a female calligrapher, and we had some conflicts while planning her exhibition. She was disappointed every time when I give her some negative reaction, so I had to be really careful not to lose her motivation. Because of similar experiences like this, I feel uncomfortable when I give a negative critique to others.

How do you handle a critique that you disagree with?
I try to objectify myself. To begin with, I embrace what I feel. It sometimes hurts me, of course, but those negative feelings don‚Äôt naturally disappear. I admit the feeling first, and then try to take it easy. Without an emotional impression, it‚Äôs easier to take the critique that I found hard to agree with. When I treat myself as a third person, I can exclude a tendency myself that I want to see only positive feedback. However, to improve myself continuously, it is important to accept both positive and negative critiques. For example, when you face a pessimistic opinion about your work, you might want to avoid taking it or feel lowered self-esteem. However, if you keep not trying to take any negative opinion, you can‚Äôt escape from every similar situation forever. You sometimes have to push and train yourself so that you can be a better person and make you work even better.

Why do you think it‚Äôs important to give and receive critiques?
Exchanging critique is very important in that the process helps us improved. I trust in Hegel‚Äôs dialectic theory. When an issue is raised, there should be any positive or negative opinion. By reinforcing the good ones and making up for bad ones, we can access a better conclusion or solution to the current issue. To refer to Hegel‚Äôs dialectic, the process of development consists of three-phase: thesis-antithesis-synthesis.
Also, critique helps us to take diverse perspectives. What‚Äôs good about having different opinions is that we can solder our logical cracks which we cannot catch ourselves. We often fall into a trap of arrogance that we understand everything and don‚Äôt miss anything. Either our thought is ideal or not, taking a critique is very helpful in that we can proofread our idea or supplement what we need more.

What Screens Want

1. Where do you stand with the two ideological camps: flat and skeuo? For example, do you think one is "better" than the other or do you strongly believe in one or the other.
Although both skeuomorphism and flat design are essential and valuable, I would say flat design works better if I have to choose only one of them. Among many reasons, I would like to talk about the efficiency of user recognition in this discussion.
Yes, I think flat design has better user recognition. Others might say that skeuomorphism is better because it is more similar to ‚Äòreal things‚Äô, so users would feel more familiar with it and therefore feel easier to take the information on the screen. However, when it comes to a matter of interface where users would meet every day, it gets bloated. To be specific, everything on the screen expresses heavy attention, which means that it becomes hard to distinguish the focus point from less important aspects. Users have to glare their eyes to find what they want to access. In other words, a screen with skeuomorphism is already full of information. Think of a book consisting of every single text with a three-dimensional aspect, like the shadow, or a comic book with only real pictures. It is not fun, but most importantly, not easily readable.
On the other hand, flat design exploits the most straightforward but most powerful medium to deliver information to users: symbols. It is a social and implicit promise that represents a sign or meaning in the most compressive forms. Of course, it necessitates some time or education for users to understand and internalize a symbol. However, once they get used to the substructure, they do not have to use their time to comprehend it, just like what text or traffic signs offer.

2. What is a zoopraxiscope and how does it relate to web and interaction design? Find another example from filmmaking or another medium that has inspired digital design.
Zoopraxiscope, named by Muybridge, is a continuative series of pictures taken at intervals of a brief time. The pictures were printed on a spinning glass disc and projected one by one to show a moving picture. The author of the article argues that web and interaction design succeed filmmaking in that zoopraxiscope has led to the advent of screens. Also, he connects web and interaction design by saying that they do not belong to the realm of aesthetics but to other aspects such as time, movement, and change.
Likewise, filmmaking has impacted the development of the digital design. In addition to the case of the screen‚Äôs advent from zoopraxiscope, the form and shape of the screen have also been affected by filmmaking. For example, the filmmaking industry has been trying to create livelier movie experiences. As one of the efforts, they made 3d glasses for the more realistic movies, and now they have reached to VR or AR stage. Unlike conventional screens, now the screens have the z-axis in which users can interact with more information and contents on the same single screen. Now users can access literally to ‚Äòbehind the scene‚Äô.

3. Do you agree with that screens want "flux"? Explain why you do or do not.
Frank emphasizes the usefulness of flux by stating that ‚Äúit encompasses many of the things we take for granted in the digital realm‚Äù. I agree with his idea in terms of adaptability and economy. In my understanding, what‚Äôs good about flux is that it assigns adaptability to screens so that they can adjust themselves to its circumstance. What I mean by the term ‚Äòcircumstance‚Äô includes the shape of screens, the time they are used, or the person who uses them. Flux does not only guarantee efficiency but also provides a broader and more extended range of digital content. Accordingly, users will have a better and more diverse experience even with the same contents.
Also, a more efficient method is naturally related to a better economic condition. In digital design or web, flux reduces the maintenance costs. Once a system is set up, there are not so many things to do to maintain it. For example, if a website consists of responsive content, the developer does not have to make different compositions of the contents for different formats of screens. It saves lots of time and effort. Similarly, if there is no flux, users might have to purchase several devices that are optimized to different web formats or purposes.

1. Why does Laurel Schwulst advocate for individual websites over social media? Do you agree with her? Please explain your answer.
Laurel highlights that individuals should have their own web pages instead of ones ruled by huge social media companies. the Poetry of Tools

1. In the opening paragraph of her post, Seu asks you, the reader to join her to 'defamiliarize our tools.' What do you think she means by that? And how do you think that action relates to her use of the word poetry?
I think what Seu wants us to do by using the phrase ‚Äòdefamiliarize our tools‚Äô is that think about the tools around us in an unconventional way. We tend to think that every tool has very specific goals that give the reasons for its existence. In other words, we connect the worth of a tool with its productivity and value it depending on its usefulness.
However, when we take a step back a little from the basis, we can fully enjoy the beauty of the tools. For example, Seu states that ‚Äòtechnology is a form of language‚Äô. When it comes to language, its primary purpose is delivering linguistic information from one to another. On the other hand, there is also a very beautiful artistic aspect of language: referring to Seu‚Äôs expression, poetry. In terms of efficiency or productivity, poetry has a relatively mere worth compared to other aspects of language. Nevertheless, I think that‚Äôs not all of it. If we regard language only as a tool for data delivery, we will never see another side of its beauty. Therefore, when we try to treat technology in an unfamiliar way, I believe that we will be able to comprehend its full potential.
